## Design Context

### Users
Office staff using the system daily at their desks — employees submitting procurement requests, managers approving them, accountants tracking payments, and construction coordinators managing projects. Users are task-focused and time-constrained; they return to the same screens repeatedly throughout the workday. The interface is a work tool, not a showcase.

### Brand Personality
**Calm, structured, precise.** This is a serious internal tool for a Turkish construction and procurement group. The interface should feel like a well-run operation — trustworthy, efficient, no noise. Not corporate-bland, but not flashy either. Quiet confidence.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Dense & data-rich.** Pack more meaningful information per screen without feeling cluttered. Think Bloomberg-lite or a refined project management terminal: tables over card grids, tabular numbers, clear scannable rows, tight but breathable spacing. Avoid decorative cards that wrap single lines of text, avoid repeated borders that box in nothing, avoid gradients as decoration.

- **Typography**: Pair `Fraunces` (italic numerics/headings for character) with `IBM Plex Sans` (body — designed for data, has tabular figures, authoritative). Both load from Google Fonts.
- **Accent color**: Move away from generic `#2563eb`. Use a deep slate-indigo — `oklch(48% 0.16 258)` — authoritative, calm, slightly cooler than standard blue. In dark mode, lighten to `oklch(70% 0.14 258)`.
- **Neutrals**: Warm-tinted slates (lean slightly warm, not pure gray) to feel grounded and human.
- **Both light and dark modes** must be excellent.
- **Anti-references**: Avoid purple-gradient-on-dark AI aesthetics, glassmorphism, card grids with icon + heading + text repeated 4x, hero metric layouts with big number + gradient accent.

### Design Principles

1. **Data before decoration.** Every pixel must carry information or aid comprehension. If removing it doesn't confuse the user, remove it.
2. **One primary action per screen.** Clear hierarchy: one obvious CTA, secondary actions as text links or icon buttons, tertiary actions hidden until needed.
3. **Tables over cards for lists.** Lists of requirements, projects, items belong in tight, scannable rows — not in padded card grids that waste vertical space.
4. **Status through typography.** Color is for status (pending/accepted/declined); use it sparingly and consistently. Weight and size do the rest of the hierarchy work.
5. **Progressive disclosure.** Show what's needed now. Filters collapse. Details expand inline or open in a focused panel. Don't front-load everything.
